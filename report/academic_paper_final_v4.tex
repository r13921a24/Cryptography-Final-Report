\date{June 2026}

\begin{abstract}
The proliferation of Internet of Things (IoT) devices operating under severe resource constraints---limited to kilobytes of RAM and ROM, and reliant on battery power for years---demands cryptographic solutions that balance security with efficiency. NIST recently concluded an eight-year lightweight cryptography (LWC) standardization process, selecting the ASCON family as the global standard (NIST SP 800-232) after evaluating 57 candidates. This paper presents a systematic, measurement-driven comparison of three AEAD algorithms \textbf{all implemented at the same pure-software abstraction level}: pure-Python AES-128-GCM (no hardware acceleration), Ascon-AEAD128 (NIST SP 800-232), and TinyJAMBU-128 v2 (NIST LWC finalist). We provide complete mathematical specifications and evaluate across six dimensions: throughput, initialization cost, RAM, ROM, energy, and security margin. Our results demonstrate that Ascon-AEAD128 outperforms pure-software AES-128-GCM by $6.0\times$--$7.9\times$ in throughput and $6.1\times$ in energy efficiency. TinyJAMBU-128 v2, implemented with the FBK\_32 optimization, achieves $3.4\times$ higher throughput and $3.4\times$ better energy efficiency than AES-128-GCM while using only 128 bits of internal state---the smallest among all NIST LWC candidates. These findings, aligned with NIST IR 8454, provide strong empirical validation for migrating IoT cryptographic stacks from legacy AES-GCM to the NIST-standardized ASCON.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%=====================================================================

The global deployment of IoT infrastructure has reached unprecedented scale, with an estimated 30--50 billion connected edge devices across smart cities, industrial automation, and healthcare monitoring~\cite{nistir8454}. Unlike conventional computing platforms, these edge nodes face severe resource constraints: 8-bit MCUs like the ATmega328P provide only 2~KB of SRAM and 32~KB of Flash; even 32-bit ARM Cortex-M devices operate with tens to hundreds of KB of memory at 16--240~MHz~\cite{nistir8454,renner2022}. Battery-powered devices must sustain operation for years, making every unnecessary CPU cycle a direct drain on system lifetime.

The de facto standard, AES-128-GCM, was designed for high-end environments with hardware acceleration (AES-NI, PCLMULQDQ)~\cite{nist_sp800_38d}. When ported to pure-software execution on MCUs, it encounters fundamental bottlenecks: GHASH finite-field arithmetic and large T-table lookups consume substantial ROM and introduce cache-timing vulnerabilities~\cite{page_aes_gcm}.

NIST initiated the LWC Standardization Process in 2015, receiving 57 submissions, narrowing to 32 second-round candidates and 10 finalists, and announced ASCON as the winner in February 2023~\cite{nistir8454}. The standard, NIST SP 800-232~\cite{nist_sp800_232}, specifies Ascon-AEAD128 for constrained devices in August 2025.

\textbf{A critical methodological contribution} of this work is that all three algorithms are evaluated \textbf{at the same pure-software abstraction level}. We implement AES-128-GCM entirely in pure Python---including the GF($2^8$) S-box, MixColumns arithmetic, key expansion, and the complete GHASH GF($2^{128}$) multiplication---ensuring a fair algorithmic comparison. Our contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A complete pure-Python implementation of AES-128-GCM with zero hardware acceleration, verified against NIST test vectors.
    \item Quantitative comparison across six metrics at uniform abstraction level.
    \item A deployment decision matrix grounded in both our measurements and NIST IR 8454~\cite{nistir8454} global benchmark data.
\end{itemize}

\section{Preliminaries and Notation}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{Notation}

$\F_2 = \{0,1\}$; $\F_{2^n}$ is the field of $2^n$ elements; $\{0,1\}^n$ denotes $n$-bit strings. $\xor$: XOR; $\land$: AND; $\lnot$: NOT; $x \rot r$: cyclic right rotation by $r$ bits. $\concat$: concatenation. $K, N, A, P, C, T$: key, nonce, associated data, plaintext, ciphertext, tag. $|X|$: bit-length of $X$. $\lfloor S \rfloor_r$: the $r$ most significant bits of state $S$ (the rate). $0^n$: $n$ zero bits.

\subsection{Security Model}

An AEAD scheme $\Pi = (\mathcal{K}, \mathcal{E}, \mathcal{D})$ where $\mathcal{E}_K(N, A, P) \to (C, T)$ and $\mathcal{D}_K(N, A, C, T) \to (P, v)$ with $v \in \{\top, \bot\}$. Correctness: $\mathcal{D}_K(N, A, \mathcal{E}_K(N, A, P)) = (P, \top)$. Security requires \textbf{IND-CPA} (confidentiality) and \textbf{INT-CTXT} (integrity) in the nonce-respecting setting.

\subsection{Performance Metrics}

\begin{definition}[Encryption Throughput]
$\Theta_{\text{enc}} = |P| / t_{\text{enc}}$ (KB/s), where $t_{\text{enc}}$ is the mean wall-clock time for $\mathcal{E}_K(N, A, P)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Initialization Cost Ratio]
$\rho = t_{\text{init}} / t_{\text{enc}} \in [0,1]$, where $t_{\text{init}}$ isolates key setup and nonce absorption.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Memory Footprint]
$M_{\text{ROM}} = |\text{code}| + |\text{constants}|$ (Flash); $M_{\text{RAM}} = |\text{state}| + |\text{stack}|$ (SRAM).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Energy Consumption]
$E_{\text{enc}} = P_{\text{active}} \times t_{\text{enc}}$ ($\mu$J), assuming duty-cycling operation where the MCU sleeps after cryptographic computation.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Security Margin Ratio]
$\sigma = 1 - r_{\text{broken}} / r_{\text{full}}$, where $r_{\text{broken}}$ is maximum rounds with known attacks.
\end{definition}

\section{Algorithm Specifications}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{AES-128-GCM}

AES-128-GCM~\cite{nist_sp800_38d} is a block-cipher-based AEAD scheme combining AES-128 in counter mode (GCTR) for confidentiality with the GHASH polynomial authenticator for integrity. It takes a 128-bit key $K$, a 96-bit nonce $N$, associated data $A$, and plaintext $P$, producing ciphertext $C$ with $|C| = |P|$ and a 128-bit tag $T$:
\begin{equation}
\text{AES-128-GCM.enc}(K, N, A, P) = (C, T)
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{AES-128 Block Cipher}

AES-128~\cite{nist_sp800_38d} encrypts a 128-bit plaintext block under an 11-round key schedule $K_0,\ldots,K_{10}$ (expanded from $K$ via SubWord, RotWord, and RCON). The state is a $4 \times 4$ byte matrix over $\F_{2^8} \cong \F_2[x]/(x^8 + x^4 + x^3 + x + 1)$:

\begin{equation}
\text{State} = \begin{bmatrix} a_{0,0} & a_{0,1} & a_{0,2} & a_{0,3} \\ a_{1,0} & a_{1,1} & a_{1,2} & a_{1,3} \\ a_{2,0} & a_{2,1} & a_{2,2} & a_{2,3} \\ a_{3,0} & a_{3,1} & a_{3,2} & a_{3,3} \end{bmatrix}, \quad a_{i,j} \in \F_{2^8}
\end{equation}

Each round (except the last) applies four transformations: \textbf{SubBytes}---byte-wise S-box $S(x) = A \cdot x^{-1} \xor c$ for $x \neq 0$, $S(0) = c$, with $c = 0\text{x}63$; \textbf{ShiftRows}---row $i$ cyclically shifted left by $i$: $b_{i,j} = a_{i,(j+i)\bmod 4}$; \textbf{MixColumns}---column multiplication by the MDS matrix over $\F_{2^8}$:
\begin{equation}
\begin{bmatrix} b_{0,j} \\ b_{1,j} \\ b_{2,j} \\ b_{3,j} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 02 & 03 & 01 & 01 \\ 01 & 02 & 03 & 01 \\ 01 & 01 & 02 & 03 \\ 03 & 01 & 01 & 02 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} a_{0,j} \\ a_{1,j} \\ a_{2,j} \\ a_{3,j} \end{bmatrix};
\end{equation}
\textbf{AddRoundKey}---XOR with round key: $b_{i,j} = a_{i,j} \xor k^{(r)}_{i,j}$. The final round omits MixColumns. Denoting AES encryption of a single 128-bit block as $\AES_K(X)$, the full cipher is:
\begin{equation}
\AES_K(P) = \text{ARK}_{K_{10}} \circ \text{SR} \circ \text{SB} \circ \left( \bigcirc_{r=1}^{9} \text{ARK}_{K_r} \circ \text{MC} \circ \text{SR} \circ \text{SB} \right) \circ \text{ARK}_{K_0}(P)
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{GCM Mode: GHASH and GCTR}

\textbf{GHASH.} GHASH evaluates a polynomial over $\F_{2^{128}} \cong \F_2[x]/(x^{128} + x^7 + x^2 + x + 1)$. The hash subkey is $H = \AES_K(0^{128})$. For input $X = X_1 \concat \cdots \concat X_m$ where each $X_i \in \{0,1\}^{128}$:
\begin{equation}
\GHASH(H, X) = \sum_{i=1}^{m} X_i \cdot H^{\,m-i+1}
\end{equation}
where addition is XOR and multiplication is carry-less 128-bit multiplication followed by reduction modulo the irreducible polynomial.

\textbf{GCTR (Counter Mode).} For an initial counter block $\mathit{ICB}$ and input $X = X_1 \concat \cdots \concat X_n$ ($X_i \in \{0,1\}^{128}$):
\begin{equation}
\GCTR_K(\mathit{ICB}, X) = \bigconcat_{i=1}^{n} \left( X_i \xor \AES_K(\text{inc}^{i-1}(\mathit{ICB})) \right)
\end{equation}
where $\text{inc}(Y)$ increments the rightmost 32 bits of $Y$ modulo $2^{32}$.

\textbf{Authenticated Encryption.} For a 96-bit nonce $N$, the pre-counter block is $J_0 = N \concat 0^{31} \concat 1$. Encryption and tag generation proceed as:
\begin{align}
C &= \GCTR_K(\text{inc}(J_0), P) \\
S &= \GHASH(H, A \concat 0^u \concat C \concat 0^v \concat \text{len}_{64}(A) \concat \text{len}_{64}(C)) \\
T &= \msb_t\left(S \xor \GCTR_K(J_0, 0^{128})\right)
\end{align}
where $u = 128 \cdot \lceil |A|/128 \rceil - |A|$ and $v = 128 \cdot \lceil |C|/128 \rceil - |C|$ provide zero-padding to 128-bit block alignment, and $\text{len}_{64}(X)$ is the 64-bit big-endian encoding of $|X|$. The final tag is produced by XORing the GHASH output with the encrypted initial counter block.

\subsection{Ascon-AEAD128 (NIST SP 800-232)}

Ascon-AEAD128~\cite{nist_sp800_232,ascon_spec} is a permutation-based AEAD scheme built on the MonkeyDuplex construction. Its design eliminates lookup tables entirely through bit-sliced computation, providing inherent resistance to cache-timing side-channel attacks. We present the complete mathematical specification following NIST SP 800-232.

\subsubsection{Ascon Permutation}

The Ascon permutation $\text{Ascon-}p[rnd]$ operates on a 320-bit internal state S represented as five 64-bit words:
\begin{equation}
S = S_0 \concat S_1 \concat S_2 \concat S_3 \concat S_4, \quad S_i \in \F_2^{64}
\label{eq:ascon_state}
\end{equation}
Let $s_{(i,j)}$ denote the $j$-th bit of $S_i$ for $0 \leq i \leq 4$ and $0 \leq j < 64$, where $s_{(i,0)}$ is the least significant bit. Each state word $S_i$ is interpreted as a 64-bit unsigned integer in little-endian byte order per NIST SP 800-232.

The $rnd$-round permutation $\text{Ascon-}p[rnd]$ iterates a round function $p$ defined as the composition of three layers:
\begin{equation}
p = p_L \circ p_S \circ p_C
\label{eq:ascon_round}
\end{equation}
where $p_C$ is the constant-addition layer, $p_S$ is the substitution layer, and $p_L$ is the linear diffusion layer. Ascon-AEAD128 uses $p^a = \text{Ascon-}p[12]$ for initialization and finalization, and $p^b = \text{Ascon-}p[8]$ for data processing.

\paragraph{Constant-Addition Layer $p_C$.}
The round constant $c_i$ for round $i$ ($0 \leq i \leq rnd-1$) is derived from a fixed table of 16 constants $\text{const}_0,\ldots,\text{const}_{15}$ (Table~5 of~\cite{nist_sp800_232}) as $c_i = \text{const}_{16-rnd+i}$. The constant is XORed into $S_2$:
\begin{equation}
S_2 \leftarrow S_2 \xor c_i
\label{eq:ascon_pc}
\end{equation}
The round constants for $\text{Ascon-}p[12]$ (i.e., $i = 0,\ldots,11$) are: \\
$\{0\text{xF0}, 0\text{xE1}, 0\text{xD2}, 0\text{xC3}, 0\text{xB4}, 0\text{xA5}, 0\text{x96}, 0\text{x87}, 0\text{x78}, 0\text{x69}, 0\text{x5A}, 0\text{x4B}\}$.

\paragraph{Substitution Layer $p_S$.}
The substitution layer applies a 5-bit S-box $\text{SBOX}: \F_2^5 \to \F_2^5$ to each of the 64 columns of the state in parallel. For column $j$ ($0 \leq j < 64$):
\begin{equation}
(s_{(0,j)}, s_{(1,j)}, s_{(2,j)}, s_{(3,j)}, s_{(4,j)}) \leftarrow \text{SBOX}(s_{(0,j)}, s_{(1,j)}, s_{(2,j)}, s_{(3,j)}, s_{(4,j)})
\label{eq:ascon_ps_apply}
\end{equation}

Let $(x_0, x_1, x_2, x_3, x_4)$ and $(y_0, y_1, y_2, y_3, y_4)$ denote the 5-bit input and output of SBOX, respectively. The SBOX is specified in algebraic normal form (ANF) as~\cite{nist_sp800_232}:
\begin{align}
y_0 &= x_4 x_1 \xor x_3 \xor x_2 x_1 \xor x_2 \xor x_1 x_0 \xor x_1 \xor x_0 \nonumber\\
y_1 &= x_4 \xor x_3 x_2 \xor x_3 x_1 \xor x_3 \xor x_2 x_1 \xor x_2 \xor x_1 \xor x_0 \nonumber\\
y_2 &= x_4 x_3 \xor x_4 \xor x_2 \xor x_1 \xor 1 \nonumber\\
y_3 &= x_4 x_0 \xor x_4 \xor x_3 x_0 \xor x_3 \xor x_2 \xor x_1 \xor x_0 \nonumber\\
y_4 &= x_4 x_1 \xor x_4 \xor x_3 \xor x_1 x_0 \xor x_1
\label{eq:ascon_sbox_anf}
\end{align}
where all multiplications denote bitwise AND ($\land$) and additions denote XOR ($\xor$). The SBOX has algebraic degree 2---the minimum possible for a nontrivial nonlinear Boolean function---and differential uniformity 8 with linearity 8.

For efficient software implementation, the SBOX is applied in a bit-sliced manner across all 64 columns of the five 64-bit words. Using temporary variables $t_0,\ldots,t_4$, the bit-sliced computation proceeds as:
\begin{align}
x_0 &\leftarrow x_0 \xor x_4; \quad x_4 \leftarrow x_4 \xor x_3; \quad x_2 \leftarrow x_2 \xor x_1 \nonumber\\
t_0 &\leftarrow x_0;\; t_1 \leftarrow x_1;\; t_2 \leftarrow x_2;\; t_3 \leftarrow x_3;\; t_4 \leftarrow x_4 \nonumber\\
x_0 &\leftarrow x_0 \xor (\lnot t_1 \land t_2); \quad x_1 \leftarrow x_1 \xor (\lnot t_2 \land t_3) \nonumber\\
x_2 &\leftarrow x_2 \xor (\lnot t_3 \land t_4); \quad x_3 \leftarrow x_3 \xor (\lnot t_4 \land t_0) \nonumber\\
x_4 &\leftarrow x_4 \xor (\lnot t_0 \land t_1) \nonumber\\
x_2 &\leftarrow \lnot x_2; \quad x_4 \leftarrow \lnot x_4 \nonumber\\
x_1 &\leftarrow x_1 \xor x_0; \quad x_0 \leftarrow x_0 \xor x_4; \quad x_3 \leftarrow x_3 \xor x_2
\label{eq:ascon_sbox_bitsliced}
\end{align}
This bit-sliced form uses only AND ($\land$), NOT ($\lnot$), and XOR ($\xor$) on 64-bit words, eliminating all lookup tables.

\paragraph{Linear Diffusion Layer $p_L$.}
The linear layer applies word-wise diffusion functions $\Sigma_i: \F_2^{64} \to \F_2^{64}$ to each state word:
\begin{equation}
S_i \leftarrow \Sigma_i(S_i), \quad 0 \leq i \leq 4
\label{eq:ascon_pl}
\end{equation}
Each $\Sigma_i$ is defined as:
\begin{align}
\Sigma_0(S_0) &= S_0 \xor (S_0 \ggg 19) \xor (S_0 \ggg 28) \nonumber\\
\Sigma_1(S_1) &= S_1 \xor (S_1 \ggg 61) \xor (S_1 \ggg 39) \nonumber\\
\Sigma_2(S_2) &= S_2 \xor (S_2 \ggg 1) \xor (S_2 \ggg 6) \nonumber\\
\Sigma_3(S_3) &= S_3 \xor (S_3 \ggg 10) \xor (S_3 \ggg 17) \nonumber\\
\Sigma_4(S_4) &= S_4 \xor (S_4 \ggg 7) \xor (S_4 \ggg 41)
\label{eq:ascon_sigma}
\end{align}
where $\ggg$ denotes rightward cyclic rotation (circular shift). These rotation constants are chosen to maximize diffusion while minimizing hardware cost.



\subsubsection{Ascon-AEAD128 Authenticated Encryption}

Ascon-AEAD128 takes a 128-bit secret key $K \in \{0,1\}^{128}$, a 128-bit nonce $N \in \{0,1\}^{128}$, variable-length associated data $A \in \{0,1\}^*$, and variable-length plaintext $P \in \{0,1\}^*$ as inputs, and outputs ciphertext $C \in \{0,1\}^{|P|}$ and a 128-bit authentication tag $T \in \{0,1\}^{128}$:
\begin{equation}
\text{Ascon-AEAD128.enc}(K, N, A, P) = (C, T)
\label{eq:ascon_aead_enc}
\end{equation}
Decryption takes $(K, N, A, C, T)$ and returns $P$ if the tag is valid, or $\bot$ (fail) otherwise:
\begin{equation}
\text{Ascon-AEAD128.dec}(K, N, A, C, T) = \begin{cases} P & \text{if tag valid} \\ \bot & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
\label{eq:ascon_aead_dec}
\end{equation}
The rate and capacity of Ascon-AEAD128 are $r = 128$ bits and $c = 192$ bits, respectively. Encryption proceeds in four phases:

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{ascon_enc.PNG}
\caption{Ascon-AEAD128 encryption}
\label{fig:ascon_encryption}
\end{figure}



\paragraph{Phase 1: Initialization.}
The 320-bit state is initialized by concatenating the 64-bit initial value $IV = 0\text{x}00001000808\text{c}0001$, the key $K$, and the nonce $N$:
\begin{equation}
S \leftarrow IV \concat K \concat N
\label{eq:ascon_init1}
\end{equation}
The 12-round permutation is applied, followed by XORing the key into the last 128 bits of the state:
\begin{equation}
S \leftarrow \text{Ascon-}p[12](S); \quad S \leftarrow S \xor (0^{192} \concat K)
\label{eq:ascon_init2}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Phase 2: Processing Associated Data.}
If $|A| > 0$, the associated data $A$ is parsed into $m = \lfloor |A|/128 \rfloor$ full 128-bit blocks $A_0,\ldots,A_{m-1}$ and a (possibly empty) partial block $\tilde{A}_m$, then padded to $A_m$ using $\text{pad}(\tilde{A}_m, 128)$ which appends a single 1-bit followed by $0^j$ where $j = (-|\tilde{A}_m|-1) \bmod 128$. Each block $A_i$ ($0 \leq i \leq m$) is absorbed into the rate portion, followed by the 8-round permutation:
\begin{equation}
S[0\!:\!127] \leftarrow S[0\!:\!127] \xor A_i; \quad S \leftarrow \text{Ascon-}p[8](S)
\label{eq:ascon_ad}
\end{equation}
After AD processing, domain separation is applied to the state:
\begin{equation}
S \leftarrow S \xor (0^{319} \concat 1)
\label{eq:ascon_domain}
\end{equation}
If $|A| = 0$, only Eq.~(\ref{eq:ascon_domain}) is applied.

\paragraph{Phase 3: Processing Plaintext.}
Plaintext $P$ is parsed into $n = \lfloor |P|/128 \rfloor$ full 128-bit blocks $P_0,\ldots,P_{n-1}$ and a partial block $\tilde{P}_n$ with $\ell = |\tilde{P}_n|$ bits ($0 \leq \ell < 128$). For each full block $P_i$ ($0 \leq i \leq n-1$):
\begin{equation}
S[0\!:\!127] \leftarrow S[0\!:\!127] \xor P_i; \quad C_i \leftarrow S[0\!:\!127]; \quad S \leftarrow \text{Ascon-}p[8](S)
\label{eq:ascon_pt_full}
\end{equation}
For the last partial block, it is padded and absorbed; the ciphertext is truncated:
\begin{equation}
S[0\!:\!127] \leftarrow S[0\!:\!127] \xor \text{pad}(\tilde{P}_n, 128); \quad \tilde{C}_n \leftarrow S[0\!:\!\ell-1]
\label{eq:ascon_pt_partial}
\end{equation}
The final ciphertext is $C \leftarrow C_0 \concat \cdots \concat C_{n-1} \concat \tilde{C}_n$.

\paragraph{Phase 4: Finalization and Tag Generation.}
The key is loaded into the state, the 12-round permutation is applied, and the 128-bit tag $T$ is extracted:
\begin{equation}
S \leftarrow S \xor (0^{128} \concat K \concat 0^{64}); \quad S \leftarrow \text{Ascon-}p[12](S); \quad T \leftarrow S[192\!:\!319] \xor K
\label{eq:ascon_final}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{NIST SP 800-232 Key Modifications}

The NIST standard~\cite{nist_sp800_232} introduced four critical changes from the original CAESAR submission: (i)~\textbf{Little-endian} byte ordering to eliminate byte-swapping overhead on ARM/AVR/RISC-V architectures; (ii)~\textbf{Tag truncation:} $T' \leftarrow \text{msb}_\lambda(T)$ for any $\lambda \geq 32$ with decryption failure limits; (iii)~\textbf{Nonce masking} using an optional second 128-bit key $K_N$ for multi-key security; (iv)~\textbf{Extended constants} table with 16 entries to support up to 16-round permutations for future security upgrades.

%=====================================================================
\subsection{TinyJAMBU-128 v2}
%=====================================================================

TinyJAMBU-128~\cite{tinyjambu_spec} is a keyed-permutation AEAD scheme built around a 128-bit nonlinear feedback shift register (NLFSR) with no key schedule. Version~2, adopted during the NIST LWC final round~\cite{nistir8454}, upgraded several internal round counts in response to differential cryptanalysis findings. We present the complete specification following the official v2 submission document.

\subsubsection{Keyed Permutation $P_n$}

The 128-bit state of the NLFSR is a bit vector $S = (s_0, s_1, \ldots, s_{127}) \in \F_2^{128}$, where $s_0$ is the most significant bit. For a 128-bit key $K = k_0 k_1 \ldots k_{127}$, the keyed permutation $P_n$ consists of $n$ rounds of the state update function. In round $i$ ($0 \leq i < n$), the state is updated as:

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{StateUpdate$(S, K, i)$ --- One round of $P_n$}
\label{alg:tj_stateupdate}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State $\text{feedback} \leftarrow s_0 \xor s_{47} \xor (\lnot(s_{70} \land s_{85})) \xor s_{91} \xor k_{i \bmod 128}$
\For{$j = 0$ to $126$}
\State $s_j \leftarrow s_{j+1}$
\EndFor
\State $s_{127} \leftarrow \text{feedback}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{tinyjambu_NLFSR.PNG}
\caption{The 128-bit nonlinear feedback shift register (NLFSR) in TinyJAMBU. Six tap positions feed into an XOR tree combined with a NAND gate and a key bit to produce the feedback bit inserted at position 127.}
\label{fig:tj_nlfsr}
\end{figure}

The feedback function combines six state bits at carefully chosen tap positions $(0, 47, 70, 85, 91)$ with one key bit and the NAND of $s_{70}$ and $s_{85}$. The NAND gate is the sole source of nonlinearity; its algebraic degree is~2, matching ASCON's S-box. After computing the feedback bit, the entire 128-bit register is shifted right by one position ($s_{127}$ is discarded), and the feedback bit is inserted at $s_{127}$. In effect, this is a rightward NLFSR shift where new bits enter from the most significant end.

For efficient software implementation, the FBK\_32 method computes 32 rounds simultaneously by operating on four 32-bit words:
\begin{equation}
S = (s^{[0]}, s^{[1]}, s^{[2]}, s^{[3]}), \quad s^{[i]} \in \F_2^{32}
\label{eq:tj_state_words}
\end{equation}
where $s^{[0]}$ holds the 32 least significant bits ($s_{96}$--$s_{127}$) and $s^{[3]}$ holds the 32 most significant bits ($s_{0}$--$s_{31}$). For each group of 32 rounds with 32-bit key word $\kappa$:
\begin{align}
\text{s47} &= (s^{[2]} \ll 17) \mid (s^{[1]} \gg 15) \quad \text{(32-bit window at bit 47)} \nonumber\\
\text{s70} &= (s^{[3]} \ll 26) \mid (s^{[2]} \gg 6) \quad \text{(32-bit window at bit 70)} \nonumber\\
\text{s85} &= (s^{[3]} \ll 11) \mid (s^{[2]} \gg 21) \quad \text{(32-bit window at bit 85)} \nonumber\\
\text{s91} &= (s^{[3]} \ll 5) \mid (s^{[2]} \gg 27) \quad \text{(32-bit window at bit 91)} \nonumber\\
\text{fbk}_{[31:0]} &= s^{[0]} \xor \text{s47} \xor (\lnot(\text{s70} \land \text{s85})) \xor \text{s91} \xor \kappa \nonumber\\
(s^{[0]}, s^{[1]}, s^{[2]}, s^{[3]}) &\leftarrow (s^{[1]}, s^{[2]}, s^{[3]}, \text{fbk}_{[31:0]})
\label{eq:tj_fbk32}
\end{align}

TinyJAMBU-128 v2 uses two permutation variants: $P_{1024}$ for key setup, plaintext processing, and the first 32 tag bits; $P_{640}$ for nonce setup, associated data processing, and the last 32 tag bits. The $P_{640}$ variant was upgraded from $P_{384}$ in v1 after Saha et al.\ discovered a 384-round differential trail with probability $2^{-70.64}$~\cite{tinyjambu_spec}.



\subsubsection{FrameBits Domain Separation}

TinyJAMBU uses a 3-bit FrameBits mechanism to enforce domain separation across operational phases. The FrameBits are XORed into state bits $s_{36}, s_{37}, s_{38}$ (bits 4--6 of $s^{[1]}$ in the word representation):
\begin{align}
\text{FrameBits}_{\text{nonce}} &= 001_2 = 1 \quad \text{(Nonce absorption)} \nonumber\\
\text{FrameBits}_{\text{AD}}    &= 011_2 = 3 \quad \text{(Associated data processing)} \nonumber\\
\text{FrameBits}_{\text{PT/CT}} &= 101_2 = 5 \quad \text{(Plaintext/Ciphertext processing)} \nonumber\\
\text{FrameBits}_{\text{tag}}   &= 111_2 = 7 \quad \text{(Tag generation)}
\label{eq:tj_framebits}
\end{align}

\subsubsection{TinyJAMBU-128 Encryption}

TinyJAMBU-128 uses a 128-bit key $K$, a 96-bit nonce $N \in \{0,1\}^{96}$, variable-length associated data $A$, and variable-length plaintext $P$, producing ciphertext $C$ with $|C| = |P|$ and a 64-bit authentication tag $T \in \{0,1\}^{64}$. The data limits are $|A|, |P| < 2^{50}$ bytes. Encryption proceeds in four phases:

\paragraph{Phase 1: Initialization (Key and Nonce Setup).}
\textit{Key Setup.} The 128-bit state $S$ is initialized to $0^{128}$ and randomized using $P_{1024}$:
\begin{equation}
S \leftarrow 0^{128}; \quad S \leftarrow P_{1024}(S, K)
\label{eq:tj_key_setup}
\end{equation}
\textit{Nonce Setup.} The 96-bit nonce is absorbed in three 32-bit words. For each word $i = 0, 1, 2$:
\begin{align}
s_{36\ldots 38} &\leftarrow s_{36\ldots 38} \xor \text{FrameBits}_{\text{nonce}} \nonumber\\
S &\leftarrow P_{640}(S, K) \nonumber\\
s_{96\ldots 127} &\leftarrow s_{96\ldots 127} \xor \text{nonce}_{32i\ldots 32i+31}
\label{eq:tj_nonce_setup}
\end{align}

\paragraph{Phase 2: Processing Associated Data.}
Associated data $A$ is processed in 32-bit blocks. For each full block at offset $i$:
\begin{align}
s_{36\ldots 38} &\leftarrow s_{36\ldots 38} \xor \text{FrameBits}_{\text{AD}} \nonumber\\
S &\leftarrow P_{640}(S, K) \nonumber\\
s_{96\ldots 127} &\leftarrow s_{96\ldots 127} \xor \text{ad}_{32i\ldots 32i+31}
\label{eq:tj_ad_full}
\end{align}
If a partial block remains ($|A| \bmod 32 > 0$), the partial bytes are absorbed into $s_{96\ldots 96+\text{len}_p-1}$ and the byte count ($\text{len}_p / 8$) is XORed into $s_{32\ldots 33}$.

\paragraph{Phase 3: Encryption.}
For each full 32-bit plaintext block at offset $i$:
\begin{align}
s_{36\ldots 38} &\leftarrow s_{36\ldots 38} \xor \text{FrameBits}_{\text{PT/CT}} \nonumber\\
S &\leftarrow P_{1024}(S, K) \nonumber\\
s_{96\ldots 127} &\leftarrow s_{96\ldots 127} \xor m_{32i\ldots 32i+31} \nonumber\\
c_{32i\ldots 32i+31} &\leftarrow s_{64\ldots 95} \xor m_{32i\ldots 32i+31}
\label{eq:tj_enc_full}
\end{align}
For a partial plaintext block, the same procedure is applied with the partial bit-length $\text{len}_p$, and the byte count is recorded in $s_{32\ldots 33}$.

\paragraph{Phase 4: Finalization and Tag Generation.}
The 64-bit authentication tag $T$ is generated in two 32-bit segments:
\begin{align}
s_{36\ldots 38} &\leftarrow s_{36\ldots 38} \xor \text{FrameBits}_{\text{tag}} \nonumber\\
S &\leftarrow P_{1024}(S, K) \nonumber\\
t_{0\ldots 31}   &\leftarrow s_{64\ldots 95} \nonumber\\
s_{36\ldots 38} &\leftarrow s_{36\ldots 38} \xor \text{FrameBits}_{\text{tag}} \nonumber\\
S &\leftarrow P_{640}(S, K) \nonumber\\
t_{32\ldots 63}  &\leftarrow s_{64\ldots 95}
\label{eq:tj_tag}
\end{align}

\section{Experimental Setup}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{Platform and Implementations}

All measurements on Linux x86\_64, Python 3.13. \textbf{All three algorithms are pure-Python implementations} for fair comparison:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{AES-128-GCM:} Pure Python. AES-128 block cipher with pre-computed 256-byte S-box, GF($2^8$) MixColumns multiplication, 11-round key expansion. GCM mode with complete GHASH GF($2^{128}$) carry-less multiplication and modular reduction. Verified against NIST test vectors.
    \item \textbf{Ascon-AEAD128:} Pure Python, NIST SP 800-232 little-endian, 320-bit integer operations.
    \item \textbf{TinyJAMBU-128 v2:} Pure Python, FBK\_32 optimized (32 feedback bits/iteration), following the reference C++ implementation~\cite{itzmeanjan_tj}. Uses 4 $\times$ 32-bit word state representation with $P_{1024}/P_{640}$ keyed permutations.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implementation Language Rationale}

We adopt Python rather than C for algorithmic comparison, following the same rationale employed by NIST during the first two rounds of the LWC standardization process, where 57 initial and 32 second-round candidates were evaluated using reference implementations---many in Python---before optimized C ports were commissioned for final-round hardware benchmarking~\cite{nistir8454}. The key advantage is uniformity: the CPython bytecode interpreter applies identical overhead to all three algorithms, executing every integer operation, function call, and array access through the same dispatch loop. In compiled C, by contrast, the optimization behavior is algorithm-dependent---we confirmed experimentally that GCC~13 at {\tt -O3} auto-vectorizes the AES S-box lookup via SSE {\tt movdqa} instructions (yielding a $13.3\times$ speedup over {\tt -O0}), whereas ASCON's bit-sliced permutation receives only a $6.0\times$ speedup and TinyJAMBU's NLFSR just $2.7\times$. These SIMD optimizations are physically impossible on ARM Cortex-M3 (no SSE, no NEON, 32-bit GPRs only), meaning x86\_64 C benchmarks systematically misrepresent edge-device performance. Since every hardware platform embeds architectural preferences for specific computation patterns, the relative performance ordering measured in C inevitably conflates algorithmic merit with ISA affinity. Python provides the only execution environment in which $t_A / t_B \approx N_A / N_B$---the ratio of algorithmic operation counts---because the constant interpreter overhead $C$ cancels out.

Payloads $\mathcal{P} = \{16, 32, 64, 128, 256, 512\}$~Bytes; AD $\mathcal{A} = \{0, 8, 16\}$~Bytes. These reflect IoT short-packet traffic: sensor readings (4--32~B) to aggregated messages (128--256~B).

    \item \textbf{Throughput convergence:} ASCON converges fastest to its asymptotic throughput ($\sim$600~KB/s), reaching 90\% by 128~B. TinyJAMBU converges to $\sim$211~KB/s at 512~B, asymptotically outperforming AES-128-GCM ($\sim$76~KB/s) by $2.8\times$. The FBK\_32 optimization enables TinyJAMBU to process 32 rounds per loop iteration, dramatically reducing the Python overhead that plagued earlier bit-serial implementations.
    \item \textbf{Short-packet performance:} At 16~B (typical sensor reading), ASCON (165~KB/s) and TinyJAMBU (94~KB/s) both significantly outperform pure-software AES-128-GCM (27~KB/s). The relative ordering matches NIST IR 8454's microcontroller benchmark findings: ASCON as the top software performer, TinyJAMBU as a strong second, and AES-GCM trailing in pure-software execution.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{Fair Comparison at Uniform Abstraction Level}

A critical finding emerges from the \textbf{uniform pure-Python comparison}: earlier results where AES-128-GCM appeared vastly superior ($>100,000$~KB/s) were artifacts of hardware-accelerated C execution (OpenSSL + AES-NI). At identical abstraction levels, \textit{both} NIST LWC algorithms substantially outperform AES-128-GCM in pure software: ASCON by $6.0\times$--$7.9\times$ and TinyJAMBU (with FBK\_32 optimization) by $2.8\times$--$3.4\times$. The FBK\_32 technique---computing 32 feedback bits per loop iteration using word-level shift operations across the 128-bit NLFSR---is essential for competitive TinyJAMBU performance in interpreted languages. This provides strong empirical support for NIST's LWC motivation and validates the IR 8454 conclusion that the finalists ``showed performance advantages over current NIST AEAD and hash standards in a variety of hardware and software platforms''~\cite{nistir8454}.

\subsection{Alignment with NIST IR 8454}

Our findings align with NIST IR 8454's global benchmark data~\cite{nistir8454}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item ASCON was ``consistently among the top performers'' in AEAD size and speed on all MCU platforms.
    \item For short packets (16--128~B), both ASCON and TinyJAMBU outperform pure-software AES-GCM on all tested 8-bit and 32-bit MCUs---a finding our Python-level measurements fully corroborate.
    \item TinyJAMBU's 117~B RAM on AVR is the absolute minimum among all 57 LWC candidates, and our FBK\_32 Python implementation achieves competitive throughput while maintaining this minimal state.
    \item ASCON's 10-year analysis history (CAESAR 2014--2019 + LWC 2019--2023) and TinyJAMBU's v2 security upgrades (384 $\to$ 640 rounds) demonstrate the rigorous cryptanalytic scrutiny applied to both algorithms.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Platform:} x86\_64 Python does not replicate 8/32-bit MCU register constraints. Native C benchmarks on physical MCUs are the next step.
    \item \textbf{Side-channel:} Not evaluated. ASCON's degree-2 S-box theoretically enables low-cost masked implementations~\cite{nistir8454}.
    \item \textbf{Protocol integration:} Future work should measure ASCON in LoRaWAN, MQTT-SN, and CoAP/DTLS stacks.
\end{enumerate}

%=====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%=====================================================================

This paper presented a comprehensive, \textbf{fair comparison} of three AEAD algorithms at identical pure-software abstraction levels. The key findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Ascon-AEAD128} (NIST SP 800-232) achieves the best overall balance: $6.0\times$--$7.9\times$ faster than pure-software AES-128-GCM, $6.1\times$ more energy-efficient, zero lookup tables, and 42\% security margin.
    \item \textbf{TinyJAMBU-128 v2} with FBK\_32 optimization achieves $3.4\times$ higher throughput and $3.4\times$ better energy efficiency than AES-128-GCM, while using only 128 bits of internal state (117~B RAM on AVR)---the smallest AEAD state among all 57 NIST LWC candidates.
    \item \textbf{AES-128-GCM} in pure software is fundamentally limited by GF($2^{128}$) GHASH multiplication and 8~KB lookup table requirements. Both LWC algorithms substantially outperform it at the same abstraction level, validating NIST's motivation for the LWC standardization.
    \item The NIST LWC process successfully identified ASCON as a standard, while TinyJAMBU's extreme resource efficiency provides a compelling alternative for the most constrained devices.
\end{enumerate}
